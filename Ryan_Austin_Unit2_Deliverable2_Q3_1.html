Preliminary Assessment: Notes Based on Subject Matter Expertise¶

Year: Unless there is a consistent increase, or decrease in mosquito number over the years, it is not clear how "Year" could be used as a predictive measure for mosquito count.¶
Week: Mosquito prevalence is seasonal because year by year the weeks of the year are consistent with respect to the climatic conditions favourable to mosquito breeding. Still, "Week" is too granular and would add too much complexity and unnecessary computation for the model. "Month" would be a better proxy for mosquito prevalance time frame.¶
Address Block: A categorical variable that will have to be converted to a numeric variable. Still, the only alternatives are that mosquitos were found at the address or they were not. A given address block would be more useful for a heat map showing gradient than for a regression model predicting mosquito count.¶
Trap: Like "Address Block", "Trap ID" is a categorical variable that is more useful for a heat map than a regression model predicting mosquito count.¶
Trap Type: A given trap type may have captured more mosquitos, giving some insight into which trap to use more often, but trap type is not a logical predictor of mosquito count.¶
Date: Even more than "Week", the date timestamp down to the second is too granular and computationally intensive for a regression model. Month is the more practical predictor.¶
WNV Present was specified to not be a part of the model.¶
Species: Species is important to know if any harbour the virus more than others. Its use as a predictor of mosquito count is questionable though. Does the count of one or another species affect the count of a third? In other words, is their coexistence competitive or complementary? How does the marginal increase of one species or another (the individual species would have to represent separate weights in the model) predicted to affect mosquito count?¶
Lat: Without being paired with longitude as a coordinate , latitude is not really a credible predictor. However, since it is known that the test area is limited to the Chicago area, knowing latitude should be one of the better predictors of mosquito count.¶
Lon: Longitude is one of the better predictors of mosquito count for the same reasons as latitude, and shares the same limitation.¶
Month: The most practical predictor of when a seasonal species is most prevalent.¶

Initial Assessment¶

The initial results confirm some of the preliminary suspicions about feature usefulness based on subject matter knowledge.¶
The correlations are too weak to include any single Date, Address Block, or Trap ID in the model without including the others.¶
There are only four species, so including three (which would determine the impact of the fourth) could be relatively useful to estimate mosquito count.¶
There are only four trap types, but as stated above, using trap type to predict mosquito count is dubious.¶
Latitude, Longitude, and Month remain the most credible predictors of mosquito count, but those correlations are low as well.¶
A note about the negative correlation of Longitude: For the negative correlations, longitude was strongest, but what does it mean? "West" longitudes are "negative" relative to the Prime Meridian. The result suggests that the further west the sample point (within the Chicaco area), the higher the mosquito count.¶

Next Steps¶

Having reasoned why certain features should be used and not others, the focus from now on will be on those features.¶

Multicollinearity Post-Assessment¶

The only multicollinearity was between latitude and longitude, two features that are needed together to pinpoint location.¶
The effect could be due to the shape of the test region. Chicago is latitude "positive" (north) and longitude "negative" (west).¶
The shape of the area may slant towards north-west, meaning that locations further north (lat +ve) also happen to be further west (long -ve).¶
Both features will be kept in them model to see how R^2 is affected by removing one or the other.¶

Residual Distribution Post-Analysis¶

Both plots show an extreme left skew of the residuals.¶
The model is not capturing the full range of the variation in the data. That was evident from the 0.05 R^2¶
Factors include:¶
1. The omission of important variables,¶
2. Non-linear relationships,¶
3. Misspecification of the functional form of the model.¶
The first one was suspected from the preliminary analsis.¶
The second one was demonstrated with scatterplots and correlation analyses.¶
I cannot speak to the third possible cause without addressing the first two.¶
